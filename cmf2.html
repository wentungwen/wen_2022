August-September, 2021

Create a historical climate data site for public educational purposes.

  • Goal

    Build an informative website without drowning the user in a data tsunami while providing the best information available to get actionable insights.

  • Challenge

    Storytelling should become a primary focus in this project because displaying all data is inappropriate. And it turns to a question: how can we use storytelling to put data into context and simultaneously achieve informative and understandable experiences?

    • System concept impossible to develop in the time limit.

    The client provided a wireframe that defines the conceptual functions and the target audience - someone with meteorological majors, but I was concerned about the product feasibility and the questionable definition of the target user. The initial target audience was those with meteorological backgrounds who used the historical climate data to conduct research.

    Define the target audience through empathy interviews

    After completing a focus group, I discovered that professional people couldn't be considered as our target audience since they have multiple channels to get open data. Thus, I reset the primary users to the general public, interested in meteorology for personal interests, educating, research, or product development needs, but lack the specialized knowledge to interpret data. Based on the previous wireframe, I developed two possible types of applications and conducted a focus group: Image-based or text-based. The former is heavily graph-based, like the NASA climate change website, while the latter is more informative, like Japan Meteorological Agency.

    Insight

    I found out the users prefer comprehensible information, not "data," such as moderate interactive features with some guidance. Namely, displaying too much data and functions in one application is unbearable. They also spent significantly more time on NASA's due to the more attractive images and animations.
    (Four interviewees, 24-49 years)

    Communicating information by lowering the flexibility during the interaction

    To avoid bombarding users with information, I reduced the flexibility of operation by filtering those functions that users can use intuitively. I displayed details separately by spliting the parameters into different pages, including rainfall and temperature.

    Re-examine the wireframe based on the insights above

    Some potential problems may cause concerns. For example, the complicated functions could result in bad user experiences because the confused searching process and attribute settings could cause higher bounce rate. Furthermore, the time limit makes it hard to develop the functions and conduct complete testing thoroughly.

    Creating a product's concept that triggers people's interest

    After considering several wireframes, we finally reached a consensus by presenting high-resolution images and detailed information in the product. In the end, I selected 3-5 attributes for the basic setting to animate a simple bar chart and integrated the text, graphs, and histograms on the same page to achieve comprehensible information.

    Mockup

    1. A flexible sidebar allows new features adding in the future.
    2. Several basic setting remains in the application only.
    3. Design a simple carousel animation with essential functions to increase interaction.
    4. Bar chart complement to transfer the complete climate information.
    5. Switch different time dimensions with arrow buttons.
    6. The tiny chart visualizes key statistics and presents them reasonably.

    Result

    Although some of the functions are removed, the clients are thrilled by this highly interactive data-based website. It is also a valuable experience for me to construct the design and usability study through distilling and to synthesize user research insights. In addition, some data-side problems appeared when the developers put it into the codes, which delayed the progress a bit because of the inadequate communication. Therefore, I learned a lot of communicational techniques from this case, including well-written delivery documents and careful verbal confirmation, which are the most precious experience.
